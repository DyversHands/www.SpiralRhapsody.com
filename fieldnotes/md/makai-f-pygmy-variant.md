### Scale Character

Meditative, soulful, harmonically open. No tritone tension. Minor with a bright 2nd.

### Sound Model

Pygmy Variant — replaces the 4th and ♭6 with a natural 2nd and ♭7, removing the notes that would commit it to a single mode. Three equally valid homes: F Dorian, C Aeolian, G Phrygian. Jazzier, more open, harmonically restless. The Ding sits on the 5th degree of Bb minor[^2] — in ensemble, the instrument naturally voices as Bb Dorian.

### Sound Model Character

The Ding F3 is flanked by G3 and Ab3 — a half-step scrape that creates immediate tension. Phrases that touch both pull hard against each other and want to resolve down to F or climb away. The low conversation is unsettled; the instrument rewards players who lean into that friction rather than avoid it.

### Scale Origin

A handpan-era variation on the Pygmy Classic.[^3] Replacing the ♭6 with a natural 2nd and ♭7 opens three modal homes instead of one — and breaks any direct lineage to the African source tradition.[^4]

### Unique Voice & Timbre

Earthy and ceramic — intimate, percussive, short-decaying. Notes separate cleanly; phrases don't blur. The upper register carries a subtle shimmer as notes decay[^5] — a liveliness rather than a pure ring. Beneath every note, a D resonates in the body[^6] — a pitch foreign to the scale, adding a dark undertow that feels slightly unmoored from the melody. And the decay itself carries a barely audible F♯ ghost[^7] — the tritone of C — so notes don't fade cleanly into silence but trail off with a faint, searching tension.

### Modal Centers

**F Dorian** — `1 – 2 – ♭3 – 5 – ♭7`  
Soulful, open, jazzy minor. The Ding anchors this center — the instrument's natural home. Natural 2nd (G) beside a minor 3rd (Ab) — brighter than pure minor. Range: Full instrument.

**C Aeolian** — `1 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6`  
Natural minor, grounded, melancholic. The C→C octave span makes this center very accessible. The ♭6 (Ab) is the Aeolian signature — distinguishes it from Dorian's natural 6th. Range: C4 → C5.

**G Phrygian** — `1 – ♭2 – 4 – ♭6 – ♭7`  
Dark, tense, ancient. Spanish, Middle Eastern, and Flamenco traditions draw on this sound. The ♭2 (Ab) immediately above G is the Phrygian fingerprint — stark, unsettled tension. Range: G3 → C5.

### Compatibility

**Solo key:** F Minor — the Ding anchors here when played alone; everything resolves to F.

**Ensemble key:** Bb Minor — the 5th degree of Bb minor, fitting naturally into any Bb minor ensemble as a Bb Dorian voice.

**Fully compatible:** F Natural Minor · F Dorian · Bb Natural Minor · Ab Major

**Shared modal center:** C Natural Minor (C Aeolian) — all five notes are at home here, but the Ding is still F. The instrument visits C minor as a modal voice rather than serving it as an ensemble instrument.

**Playable with care:** Bb Minor — G natural vs G♭, raised 6th, mild color tension.

**Avoid:** A Minor (Ab flattens the root; Eb is a tritone against A) · D Minor (Ab is a tritone against D; Eb is a flat 2nd against D)

**Plays well with:**
- *Panart Hang G Pygmy* — 3 shared notes (G, C, Eb) in C Minor. Ab and Bb both sit naturally in C Natural Minor. Zero clashes in that context.
- *Pantheon Halo C XXD* — 5 shared notes (F, G, Ab, C, Eb) in F Dorian / C Minor. XXD contains all 5 Pygmy Variant notes plus D. Zero clashes. D is the natural 6th of F Dorian — additive, not conflicting.

[^1]: Hydroforming uses high-pressure fluid to press a steel blank into a mold, producing a very consistent and symmetrical shell geometry. The 53 cm diameter places the primary wave interference zone in the upper register around Bb4.
[^2]: When a handpan's Ding sits on the 5th of a minor key, the instrument slots into ensemble as a Dorian voice for that key. Dorian shares all notes with natural minor but has a raised 6th, giving it a brighter, more outward-facing character. The F Ding is the 5th of Bb minor — so in a Bb minor ensemble, this instrument contributes a Bb Dorian flavor even while playing in its own F-rooted mode.
[^3]: The Pygmy Classic derives from Ba-Benzélé vocal tradition via the Panart Hang. The Variant is a derivative scale developed within the handpan world, retaining the same pentatonic interval fingerprint but shifted to a different modal rotation. Also known as Dorian Pentatonic and Man Gong.
[^4]: Whether the Variant has any independent cultural origin is not established. It is best understood as a modal reframing of the Classic — same DNA, different tonal center of gravity.
[^5]: The 53 cm shell creates wave interference centered around Bb4. Ab4 sits just below this zone and rings cleanly; C5 sits above it. The result is a subtle asymmetry in the upper register — Ab4 is pure, C5 carries a faint shimmer or wobble in the sustain. This is a character trait of this shell size, not a flaw.
[^6]: The Gu port acts as a Helmholtz resonator — air oscillating in and out adds a low cavity tone beneath every note. Here it is tuned to D2, a pitch not in the Pygmy Variant scale, so it adds a slightly detached dark undertow rather than reinforcing the root. It is felt more than heard.
[^7]: The steel shell carries a faint F♯2/F♯3 ghost resonance — a structural artifact, not a tuned element. F♯ is the tritone of C, one of the instrument's central notes. When C is played, a barely audible tritone shimmer appears briefly in the decay before dissolving.
