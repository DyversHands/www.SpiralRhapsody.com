# Pantheon Steel Halo C XXD

**Maker:** Kyle Cox · **Date:** August 2009 · **Material:** Nitrided steel  
**Serial:** 14 · **Maker's Mark:** Signed by Kyle Cox · **Generation:** Genesis

Kyle Cox and several other makers in the handpan community have cited this instrument — serial #14 — as one of his early masterworks.

### Scale Character

Deep, resonant, and grounded. Six notes rather than five gives it more melodic vocabulary than the Pygmy instruments — more linear, less circular.

### Sound Model

Xiao Xiong Diao — C Natural Minor hexatonic, the full minor scale minus the ♭7. Its structure follows Chinese hexatonic tradition[^18], though the scale itself is original to Pantheon Steel. One clear home: C Aeolian. The natural 2nd (D) and ♭6 (Ab) together give it a distinctly melancholic, eastern character — darker than the Pygmy Variant, more melodically complete than the Classic.

### Sound Model Character

The Ding C3 sits a perfect 4th below F3 — a wide drop that gives it deep, isolated gravity. Two low conversations are available. Starting from F3 opens the ensemble voice: F→G→Ab rises with a whole-step then a half-step, the natural Dorian opening — outward-facing, collaborative, ready to lock into an ensemble. Starting from G3 opens the solo voice: the G→Ab half-step scrapes immediately into Phrygian territory — restless, inward, self-contained. The Ding anchors both but belongs fully to neither.

### Scale Origin

Coined by Kyle Cox of Pantheon Steel[^19] — 小熊调 (Xiǎo Xióng Diào) follows the classical Chinese diào naming convention and translates as "Little Bear Mode."[^20] The scale is a Chinese hexatonic: the minor pentatonic expanded by one color tone.

### Unique Voice & Timbre

Deep, ceramic, and resonant — the largest and lowest-voiced of the three instruments, with a baritone weight that the Pygmy instruments don't approach. Notes sustain with warmth and projection[^21]; the wide shell gives each strike room to breathe. The upper register is unusually clean[^22] — the scale sits below the interference zone rather than grazing it. The body adds a deep cavity tone[^23] beneath the melody, lower than the Makai's, which anchors each phrase with a felt rather than heard foundation.

### Modal Centers

**C Aeolian** — `1 – 2 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6`  
Deep natural minor, hexatonic. The Ding anchors this center firmly. More melodically complete than the Pygmy scales — six steps give more linear options. The ♭6 (Ab) and natural 2nd (D) together define the eastern, melancholic quality of the scale. Range: Full instrument.

**F Dorian** — `1 – 2 – ♭3 – 5 – 6 – ♭7`  
Soulful and open. In ensemble with F minor instruments, the C Ding voices as the 5th — the same relationship the Pygmy instruments have with their ensemble keys. Natural 6th (D) beside a minor 3rd (Ab) — the Dorian brightness emerging from an otherwise dark scale. Range: F3 → F4 (and above).

**Ab Lydian** — `1 – 3 – ♯4 – 5 – 6 – 7`  
Luminous, floating, otherworldly. The D natural becomes a sharp 4th against Ab — the Lydian fingerprint. Unusual but accessible on this instrument. The ♯4 (D) above Ab is pure Lydian — bright, suspended, unresolved upward tension. Range: Ab3 → G4.

### Compatibility

**Solo key:** C Minor — the Ding anchors here when played alone; the instrument's six notes all resolve naturally to C as the tonal center.

**Ensemble key:** F Minor — the 5th degree of F minor, fitting naturally into any F minor ensemble as an F Dorian voice.

**Fully compatible:** C Natural Minor · C Aeolian · F Dorian · Eb Major · Ab Major

**Playable with care:** G Minor — Ab is a flat 2nd against G. One clash, mild color tension.

**Avoid:** D Minor (Eb is a flat 2nd against D; Ab is a tritone against D) · A Minor (Eb is a tritone against A; Ab directly flattens the root)

**Plays well with:**
- *Makai F Pygmy Variant* — 5 shared notes (F, G, Ab, C, Eb) in F Dorian / C Minor. Contains all 5 Pygmy Variant notes. Zero clashes. The D on the XXD fills the one degree the F Pygmy lacks, expanding ensemble vocabulary without tension.
- *Panart Hang G Pygmy* — 4 shared notes (G, C, D, Eb) in C Minor. Bb (Panart) is the ♭7 of C minor; F and Ab (XXD) are both in C Natural Minor. Zero clashes.

[^17]: Pantheon Steel's Halo Genesis shells are shaped using a rolling process rather than hydroforming or hand sinking, leaving characteristic horizontal striations visible on the surface. The 58 cm diameter is larger than most handpans, contributing to the baritone register and extended sustain.
[^18]: Chinese music theory builds hexatonic scales by adding one color tone to a pentatonic root, splitting a minor third into a half-step and a whole step. The XXD does exactly this: it takes the Yu pentatonic (minor pentatonic: 1–♭3–4–5–♭6) and adds the 2nd as a color tone. Kyle Cox followed this structural logic when creating the scale, but Xiao Xiong Diao is his own invention — the name does not appear in Chinese musicology outside the handpan world.
[^19]: Kyle Cox created this tuning for the Genesis Halo and named it in the style of Chinese modal naming: descriptive character + diào (scale). Related Pantheon scales in the bear family: Da Xiong Diao (Big Bear Mode, heptatonic) and Ursa Minor.
[^20]: 小 (xiǎo) = small/little, 熊 (xióng) = bear, 调 (diào) = scale/mode. The scale is structurally grounded in Chinese hexatonic tradition: the Yu pentatonic (minor pentatonic: 1 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6) expanded by one color tone (the 2nd), splitting a minor third into a half-step and a whole step — exactly how Chinese music theory derives hexatonic scales from pentatonic roots.
[^21]: The 58 cm Halo Genesis shell is formed using Pantheon Steel's distinctive rolled-steel technique — visible as characteristic horizontal striations on the shell surface. Larger and heavier than most handpans, Pantheon described the Genesis as a baritone: its lowered register creates warmth and projection that smaller shells can't produce.
[^22]: The wave interference zone on a 58 cm shell falls around Ab4–A4. The XXD's highest note is G4 — just below that threshold. The fundamentals of all eight tone fields are clear of the primary interference range, which contributes to the instrument's reputation for coherence and purity across its full register.
[^23]: The larger 58 cm cavity with a deep port produces a Helmholtz tone at C2 — the same pitch class as the Ding, two octaves below. Unlike the Makai's D2 (a pitch foreign to its scale), C2 is in direct harmonic alignment with the instrument's root. The cavity bloom reinforces the tonal center from below rather than adding an unmoored undertow.
